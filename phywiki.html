Q1
A particle moves under a central force F(r) = −kr. The orbit of the particle is an ellipse with the force centre at the centre (not focus) of the ellipse. The time period T of the orbit depends on the semi-major axis a and semi-minor axis b as:
Classical Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
For F = −kr (linear restoring force), both x and y equations reduce to simple harmonic motion: ẍ = −(k/m)x and ÿ = −(k/m)y. Since ω is the same in both directions, the period T = 2π√(m/k), which is independent of a and b. The orbit is an ellipse centered at the force centre (unlike the Kepler case), and the period has no dependence on the shape of the ellipse.
Q2
The ground state energy of a particle in an infinite square well of width L is E₁. If the width is suddenly doubled to 2L, what is the probability that the particle is found in the ground state of the new well?
Quantum Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
The overlap integral ⟨ψ₁(new)|ψ₁(old)⟩ = ∫₀ᴸ √(2/2L) sin(πx/2L) · √(2/L) sin(πx/L) dx. Evaluating this gives 8√2 / (3π) ≈ 0.9003. The probability is the square of this: P = (8√2/3π)² ≈ 0.81 or 81%. The wave function of the old ground state has significant overlap with the new ground state because both are concentrated in the left half of the new well.
Q3
For a canonical ensemble, the Helmholtz free energy F is related to the partition function Z by F = −kT ln Z. For a 3D harmonic oscillator at high temperature, F scales with T as:
Stat. Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
At high T, the 3D harmonic oscillator partition function Z = (kT/ℏω)³ (classical limit). Therefore F = −kT ln Z = −3kT ln(kT/ℏω). Expanding: F = −3kT ln(kT) + 3kT ln(ℏω). The dominant term is F ∝ −T ln T, so F decreases roughly linearly with T (with a logarithmic correction). The entropy S = −∂F/∂T = 3k[1 + ln(kT/ℏω)] grows logarithmically.
Q4
An electromagnetic wave is incident normally on an interface between two media with refractive indices n₁ and n₂. The reflectance R is given by R = [(n₁−n₂)/(n₁+n₂)]². For n₁ = 1 and n₂ = 1.5, calculate R.
Electrodynamics
✦ Answer & Solution
Substituting directly: R = [(1 − 1.5)/(1 + 1.5)]² = [−0.5/2.5]² = [−0.2]² = 0.04 or 4%. This means 4% of the incident intensity is reflected at an air-glass interface, which matches the experimental observation (~4% for each surface of an optical lens). This is the Fresnel reflectance formula for normal incidence derived from Maxwell's boundary conditions.
Q5
The Fermi energy of copper is 7 eV. Estimate the velocity of electrons at the Fermi level. (mₑ = 9.1×10⁻³¹ kg)
Solid State
✦ Answer & Solution
Using EF = ½mₑvF², we get vF = √(2EF/mₑ). EF = 7 eV = 7 × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J = 1.12×10⁻¹⁸ J. vF = √(2 × 1.12×10⁻¹⁸ / 9.1×10⁻³¹) = √(2.46×10¹²) ≈ 1.57 × 10⁶ m/s. This is ~0.5% of the speed of light, validating the non-relativistic treatment for copper electrons.

Q1
The Hamiltonian of a system is H = p²/2m + ½mω²x² + λx⁴. Using first-order perturbation theory, the correction to the ground state energy is:
Quantum Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
The perturbation is H' = λx⁴. The first-order correction is E¹ = ⟨0|λx⁴|0⟩. Using x = √(ℏ/2mω)(a† + a) and the identity ⟨0|x⁴|0⟩ = 3(ℏ/2mω)², we get E¹ = 3λℏ²/4m²ω². The calculation uses ⟨0|(a†+a)⁴|0⟩ = 3, which comes from counting all contractions using Wick's theorem.
Q2
In the Lagrangian L = ½m(ẋ² + ẏ²) − V(x,y), if V is independent of x (i.e., ∂V/∂x = 0), what is the conserved quantity and its physical interpretation?
Classical Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
From the Euler–Lagrange equation, ∂L/∂x = 0 implies d/dt(∂L/∂ẋ) = 0, so ∂L/∂ẋ = mẋ = constant. The conserved quantity is the x-component of linear momentum pₓ = mẋ. By Noether's theorem, the translational symmetry in x (cyclic coordinate) gives conservation of the conjugate momentum. This is a direct application of the connection between symmetry and conservation laws.
Q3
A nucleus undergoes beta decay: ⁶⁰Co → ⁶⁰Ni + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ. The Q-value of this decay is 2.82 MeV. What type of beta decay is this and what is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electron?
Nuclear Physics
✦ Answer & Solution
This is β⁻ decay (neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino), as Z increases from 27 to 28. The maximum kinetic energy of the electron equals the Q-value minus the recoil energy of the daughter nucleus (negligible for heavy nuclei). So KE_max ≈ Q = 2.82 MeV. In practice, the electron energy spectrum is continuous from 0 to Q, with the neutrino carrying the remaining energy — this was Pauli's key insight that led to the neutrino hypothesis.
Q4
The residue of f(z) = e^z / z³ at z = 0 is:
Mathematical Physics
✦ Answer & Solution
Expand eᶻ as a Taylor series: eᶻ = 1 + z + z²/2! + z³/3! + ... So f(z) = eᶻ/z³ = z⁻³ + z⁻² + (1/2)z⁻¹ + 1/6 + ... The residue is the coefficient of z⁻¹, which is 1/2. By the residue theorem, the contour integral ∮f(z)dz = 2πi × (1/2) = πi. This is a pole of order 3 at z = 0.

Q1
The critical temperature Tᶜ for Bose–Einstein condensation in 3D for N bosons in a box of volume V is proportional to:
Stat. Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
For BEC, the condition is n = N/V = 2.612/λ³_dB where λ_dB = h/√(2πmkT). Solving for T: Tᶜ = (h²/2πmk)(N/2.612V)^(2/3). So Tᶜ ∝ (N/V)^(2/3) ∝ n^(2/3). Higher number density means higher Tᶜ. For ⁸⁷Rb atoms in typical BEC experiments, this gives Tᶜ ~ 100 nK, consistent with experiments first achieved by Cornell and Wieman in 1995.
Q2
Using the Born approximation, the differential scattering cross-section dσ/dΩ for a Yukawa potential V(r) = V₀e^(−μr)/r is:
Quantum Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
In Born approximation, f(θ) = −(2m/ℏ²) × (1/4π) ∫V(r)e^(iq·r)d³r where q = 2k sin(θ/2). The Fourier transform of V₀e^(−μr)/r is 4πV₀/(q² + μ²). So f(θ) = −(2mV₀/ℏ²)/(q² + μ²) and dσ/dΩ = (2mV₀/ℏ²)² × 1/(4k²sin²(θ/2) + μ²)². As μ → 0, this reduces to the Coulomb (Rutherford) scattering formula.
Q3
In a p-type semiconductor, the Hall coefficient RH is measured to be negative. Is this statement true or false? Explain.
Solid State
✦ Answer & Solution
False. The Hall coefficient RH = 1/(pq) for a p-type semiconductor, where p is the hole concentration and q is the elementary charge (positive). Since p > 0 and q > 0, RH is positive for p-type semiconductors. For n-type, the majority carriers are electrons (negative charge), so RH = −1/(nq) is negative. The sign of RH is a direct experimental way to determine the type of majority carriers in a semiconductor.

3 QUESTIONS

Q1
A Carnot engine operates between temperatures T₁ = 800 K and T₂ = 300 K. What is its efficiency, and if it absorbs 1000 J from the hot source, how much work does it deliver?
Thermodynamics
✦ Answer & Solution
Carnot efficiency η = 1 − T₂/T₁ = 1 − 300/800 = 1 − 0.375 = 0.625 or 62.5%. Work delivered W = η × Q₁ = 0.625 × 1000 = 625 J. Heat rejected to cold reservoir Q₂ = Q₁ − W = 375 J. The Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible for any engine operating between these temperatures — a consequence of the second law of thermodynamics.
Q2
The commutator [Lx, Ly] equals iℏLz. Using this, find [L², Lz] where L² = Lx² + Ly² + Lz².
Quantum Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
[L², Lz] = [Lx² + Ly² + Lz², Lz] = [Lx², Lz] + [Ly², Lz] + [Lz², Lz]. [Lz², Lz] = 0. [Lx², Lz] = Lx[Lx,Lz] + [Lx,Lz]Lx = Lx(−iℏLy) + (−iℏLy)Lx = −iℏ(LxLy + LyLx). [Ly², Lz] = Ly[Ly,Lz] + [Ly,Lz]Ly = Ly(iℏLx) + (iℏLx)Ly = iℏ(LyLx + LxLy). Adding: [L², Lz] = 0. This means L² and Lz share simultaneous eigenstates |l,m⟩.
Q3
The Debye temperature of copper is θD = 343 K. At very low temperatures (T ≪ θD), the heat capacity Cv scales as:
Solid State
✦ Answer & Solution
In the Debye model at low temperatures (T ≪ θD), only long-wavelength acoustic phonons are thermally excited. The heat capacity follows the Debye T³ law: Cv = (12π⁴Nk/5)(T/θD)³ ∝ T³. For copper at T = 10 K: Cv/Nk ≈ (12π⁴/5)(10/343)³ ≈ 0.0025. Including the electronic contribution (linear in T), total Cv = γT + AT³, and plotting Cv/T vs T² gives a straight line whose intercept is γ (Sommerfeld coefficient).

Q1
Two inertial frames S and S' move with relative velocity v = 0.6c along x-axis. A rod of proper length L₀ = 2 m is at rest in S'. What is its length as measured in S?
Classical Mech.
✦ Answer & Solution
Length contraction: L = L₀/γ where γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) = 1/√(1 − 0.36) = 1/√0.64 = 1/0.8 = 1.25. So L = 2/1.25 = 1.6 m. The rod appears shorter in the frame where it is moving. Note: the contraction is only along the direction of motion; transverse dimensions are unaffected. This is a consequence of the relativity of simultaneity, not a real physical compression.
Q2
An op-amp in inverting configuration has R_f = 100 kΩ and R_in = 10 kΩ. What is the closed-loop gain?
Electronics
✦ Answer & Solution
For an ideal op-amp in inverting configuration, the closed-loop gain is Av = −Rf/Rin = −100k/10k = −10. The negative sign indicates phase inversion of 180°. The virtual ground concept: the inverting input is at virtual ground (≈0 V) due to negative feedback and high open-loop gain. The input current i = Vin/Rin all flows through Rf, giving Vout = −i×Rf = −(Rf/Rin)×Vin.
Q3
The binding energy per nucleon is maximum for iron-56 (⁵⁶Fe) at ~8.8 MeV/nucleon. Explain qualitatively why nuclei lighter or heavier than ⁵⁶Fe have lower binding energy per nucleon.
Nuclear Physics
✦ Answer & Solution
For light nuclei: the surface term dominates — a larger fraction of nucleons are on the surface with fewer neighbours, reducing binding. For heavy nuclei: Coulomb repulsion between protons grows as Z², while the short-range strong force saturates. Beyond ⁵⁶Fe, the Coulomb penalty per nucleon exceeds any gain from adding nucleons, reducing BE/A. This is why fusion is energetically favourable for light nuclei (releasing energy going toward ⁵⁶Fe) and fission is favourable for heavy nuclei (releasing energy by splitting toward ⁵⁶Fe).

2 QUESTIONS

Q1
A 2D electron gas has a constant density of states g(E) = m/πℏ². Find the Fermi energy at T = 0 for electron density n (per unit area).
Solid State
✦ Answer & Solution
At T = 0, all states up to EF are filled: n = ∫₀^EF g(E) dE = (m/πℏ²) × EF. Solving: EF = πℏ²n/m. In 2D, the constant DOS means EF is simply proportional to n — unlike 3D where EF ∝ n^(2/3). This is a key feature of 2D materials like graphene (where DOS is linear in E, not constant) and has important implications for quantum Hall effect physics.
Q2
Legendre polynomial P₂(x) = ½(3x²−1). Verify the orthogonality: ∫₋₁¹ P₁(x)P₂(x)dx = 0, where P₁(x) = x.
Mathematical Physics
✦ Answer & Solution
∫₋₁¹ P₁(x)P₂(x)dx = ∫₋₁¹ x · ½(3x²−1)dx = ½∫₋₁¹(3x³−x)dx. Now 3x³ is an odd function and x is also odd — both are odd functions. The integral of any odd function over [−1,1] is zero. Therefore ∫₋₁¹ P₁P₂ dx = 0 ✓. General orthogonality: ∫₋₁¹ Pₗ(x)Pₗ'(x)dx = 2/(2l+1) × δₗₗ'. This underlies the multipole expansion in electrostatics and quantum mechanics (spherical harmonics).
